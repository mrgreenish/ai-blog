Benchmarks tell you what a model *can* do. Personality tells you what it *will* do when you don't spell out every detail. Two models with identical scores can produce wildly different results — not because one is smarter, but because one interprets ambiguity differently, takes more initiative, or defaults to a different level of caution.

## Gemini: the careful one

<ModelLabels provider="Google" tier="Conservative" bestFor="Production refactors" />

Gemini asks for permission. It's conservative, sticks close to what you asked for, and rarely goes off-script. Ambiguous task? It'll ask a clarifying question rather than assume and run.

**Literal-minded.** Gemini does what you say, not what you might have meant. Ask it to "clean up this component" and it'll fix formatting — it won't restructure the hierarchy or suggest a different pattern unless you ask.

**Risk-averse.** Multiple valid approaches? Gemini picks the safest, most conventional one. Reliable for production work, but you miss out on solutions that require a judgment call.

**Consistent under pressure.** In long sessions with large contexts, Gemini stays level. It doesn't drift or get "creative" with your architecture. For marathon refactoring, this steadiness matters.

**Where it falls short.** It won't push back on your approach, suggest alternatives, or notice you're solving the wrong problem. Reliable executor, not a thought partner.

## GPT-5.3-Codex: the balanced one

<PersonalFavorite />

<ModelLabels provider="OpenAI" tier="Balanced" bestFor="Everyday shipping" />

GPT sits in the middle — initiative when the situation is clear, deference when it's ambiguous. Output that feels sensible without being surprising. The 5.3 update shifted Codex into a general-purpose agent: 25% faster, fewer tokens, and it can now run autonomously for hours.

**Pragmatic defaults.** GPT picks the approach most developers would pick. Stack Overflow's accepted answer energy — not the most clever, but the one your team will understand.

**Measured initiative.** Fills in reasonable gaps — default error handling, obvious edge cases, standard patterns — but stops short of architectural decisions. High hit rate of "helpful without overstepping."

**Good at reading the room.** Detailed prompt? GPT stays precise. Loose prompt? It makes reasonable assumptions but flags them. It adapts its initiative to match your specificity.

**Predictable output format.** The most consistent at following format instructions. "Respond in JSON" or "only output code" — it complies where other models can't help adding commentary.

**Where it falls short.** Rarely surprises you with insight. It gives you what you asked for, not necessarily what you need. With 5.3's agentic capabilities, autonomy is powerful but guardrails matter.

## Claude Sonnet 4.6: the proactive one

<PersonalFavorite />

<ModelLabels provider="Anthropic" tier="Proactive" bestFor="Feature design" />

Claude has opinions and isn't shy about sharing them. It pushes back on your approach, suggests alternatives, notices bugs you didn't ask about, and restructures code to be "better" — even when you just wanted a simple change. With 4.6, Sonnet scores within 1.2 points of Opus on SWE-bench at one-fifth the cost, with a 1M token context window and automatic compaction for long sessions.

**Creative by instinct.** It might suggest a better API surface, flag that your data model will break at scale, or restructure code in a way you hadn't considered.

**Proactive to a fault.** While implementing a feature, it'll spot naming inconsistencies, missing error boundaries, potential race conditions in adjacent code — and often fix them without being asked. Magical in short sessions. A 40-file diff in long ones.

**Opinionated about code quality.** Strong preferences about structure, naming, and patterns. It will refactor code to match its taste. Setting explicit constraints ("do not refactor unless I ask") is essential.

**Excellent at explaining.** The clearest, most useful explanations of any model. It connects your specific code to the general principle. This is where the personality really shines.

**Where it falls short.** Scope creep. Its instinct to be helpful means it expands tasks. The fix is explicit constraints in your prompt or `CLAUDE.md`.

## Claude Opus 4.6: the deep thinker

<PersonalFavorite />

<ModelLabels provider="Anthropic" tier="Deep" bestFor="Hard problems & architecture" />

Opus isn't Sonnet with the volume up. Where Sonnet notices things, Opus understands them — tracing actual logic rather than pattern-matching, reasoning about systems rather than files. The 4.6 update sharpened it considerably: industry-leading agentic coding, 1M token context window, and long-context retrieval that jumped from 18.5% to 76%.

**Deep accuracy.** Where other models pattern-match, Opus traces actual logic. It catches bugs three levels of indirection deep, identifies race conditions by simulating concurrent execution, and spots type issues TypeScript misses.

**Architecturally creative.** It suggests different abstractions entirely and explains why your current approach will cause problems two features from now. It thinks in systems, not just code.

**Thorough to the point of slow.** Considers more options, explores more edge cases, gives more complete answers. Overkill for quick tasks. For hard problems and architecture decisions, the thoroughness pays for itself.

**Proactive with substance.** Where Sonnet might fix a naming inconsistency, Opus notices your abstraction is leaking, explains why, and suggests a restructure that fixes the root cause.

**Where it falls short.** Cost and latency. For routine tasks — scaffolding, simple refactors, boilerplate — you're burning money without proportional value. Reserve it for problems that actually need it.

## Cursor Composer-1: the focused one

<PersonalFavorite />

<ModelLabels provider="Cursor" tier="Focused" bestFor="Targeted edits" />

Composer-1 is the IDE-native model. It knows your open files, recent diffs, and cursor position. That context is its edge — no pasting code into a chat window.

**Fast.** Responses come back in seconds, not tens of seconds. That speed keeps you in flow — you don't context-switch, don't lose your train of thought. For the rapid, incremental work that makes up most of a coding day, this matters more than raw capability.

**Tight scope by design.** Optimized for targeted edits — fix this function, rename this prop, implement this interface. It executes cleanly without wandering. The model least likely to touch things you didn't ask about.

**Fast iteration loop.** Embedded in the editor, the round-trip from prompt to applied diff is instant. Accept or reject hunks, move on. Chain ten small tasks in the time another model takes to respond once.

**Where it falls short.** The speed comes at the cost of depth. It doesn't have 1.5's thinking mode — it won't pause to reason through ambiguity or second-guess an approach mid-task. For anything where the right path requires judgment under uncertainty, you'll want 1.5.

## Cursor Composer-1.5: the agentic one

<ModelLabels provider="Cursor" tier="Agentic" bestFor="End-to-end tasks" />

Composer-1.5 doesn't just edit files — it acts. Terminal commands, reading output, making more edits, looping until the task is done. The closest thing to an AI developer that can execute end-to-end.

**Multi-step execution.** Give it a task spanning multiple files with verification — "add this feature, make sure tests pass, fix type errors" — and it works through the steps autonomously. It runs the build, reads errors, fixes them, and reruns.

**Self-correcting.** It observes the results of its own actions, catches mistakes a non-agentic model would leave for you. Sees the TypeScript error, understands it in context, fixes it — no copy-pasting errors back into a prompt.

**Edits across the whole codebase.** Not limited to open files. It navigates the project, finds relevant files, and makes coordinated changes across many of them.

**Where it falls short.** Autonomy has a cost. It can go down wrong paths and make many changes before you realize it's off track. Short task scopes and frequent checkpoints are essential.

## How personality affects prompting

The pattern is consistent: match your prompt style to the model's instincts. Be explicit with Gemini — it won't infer intent. Set hard scope limits with Claude — "mention issues but don't fix them" — or its helpfulness will expand your task. Give Composer-1.5 a clear success condition and let it run, but check in at breakpoints.

The right model isn't the smartest one — it's the one whose personality matches the task.
